# global-fisheries-tradeoff-analysis

# Global Fisheries Sustainability Decision

## Decision Statement
Should an international fisheries management organization prioritize stricter catch regulations or maintain current harvesting levels to support economic stability?

---

## Executive Summary

Global fisheries are a critical source of food, employment, and economic activity, supporting millions of people worldwide. However, increasing fishing intensity has placed significant pressure on marine ecosystems, leading to declining fish populations and long-term sustainability concerns. This creates a fundamental tension between short-term economic gains and long-term ecological stability.

The decision facing global fisheries regulators is complex. On one hand, stricter regulations—such as catch limits, quotas, and marine protected areas—can help rebuild fish stocks and preserve ecosystems. On the other hand, these policies may reduce immediate income for fishers and disrupt economies that depend heavily on fishing industries. Maintaining current harvesting levels may support economic stability in the short term but risks long-term depletion of resources.

This project analyzes global fisheries data to support decision-making by identifying patterns in fish population trends, catch volumes, and environmental impacts. The goal is to provide evidence that helps policymakers evaluate trade-offs and choose a sustainable path forward.

## Explanation of Key Feedback Loops

### Reinforcing Loop (R): Overfishing Cycle
- Fishing Intensity (+) → Catch Volume  
- Catch Volume (+) → Fisher Income  
- Fisher Income (+) → Fishing Intensity  

This reinforcing loop creates a snowball effect where increased fishing activity drives higher income, which further incentivizes more fishing, accelerating resource depletion.

### Balancing Loop (B): Sustainability Control
- Fishing Intensity (–) → Fish Population  
- Fish Population (+) → Ecosystem Health  
- Ecosystem Health (+) → Regulation Strictness  
- Regulation Strictness (–) → Fishing Intensity  

This balancing loop acts to stabilize the system by increasing regulation when ecosystem health declines, reducing fishing pressure over time.
